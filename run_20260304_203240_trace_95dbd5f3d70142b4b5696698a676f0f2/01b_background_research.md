Across disciplines, “intelligence” is treated less as a single essence and more as a cluster of capacities—goal-directed behavior, learning, adaptation, reasoning, and problem solving—expressed at different levels of description. Psychology and psychometrics operationalize intelligence via stable individual differences and test structures (e.g., broad abilities models such as CHC), while cognitive science and neuroscience often emphasize mechanisms (attention, working memory, predictive processing, large-scale networks) and embodied, context-sensitive control. Philosophy of mind presses on conceptual boundaries—whether intelligence requires representation, consciousness, rationality, or norm-governed action—highlighting that definitions often encode theoretical commitments about what should “count” as intelligent behavior. In AI and theoretical computer science, foundational work attempts to formalize intelligence in agentic terms. Legg & Hutter synthesize many informal definitions into a widely cited core idea—an agent’s ability to achieve goals across a wide range of environments—and propose an associated formalism (“universal intelligence”) grounded in algorithmic information theory (Kolmogorov complexity) and expected reward across environments. This approach motivates “universal psychometrics” and practical approximations/tests, but also attracts critiques: formal definitions may depend heavily on chosen environment classes, reward formulations, and idealizations that are hard to realize or validate empirically. In parallel, information-theoretic/neuroscientific frameworks like Friston’s free-energy principle argue for unifying accounts of perception-action and learning as minimizing variational free energy (bounding surprise) under generative models—suggesting a different route to unification that links cognition to inference and control.
