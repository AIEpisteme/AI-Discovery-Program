Across disciplines, **intelligence** is treated less as a single “thing” and more as a family of capacities: acquiring information from the environment, learning from experience, forming internal representations, reasoning/planning under uncertainty, and **adapting** behavior to achieve goals. In mainstream AI, Russell & Norvig frame the field around designing **rational agents**—systems that perceive and act to maximize expected goal achievement—while also reviewing alternative “human-like” criteria such as the **Turing Test** (an operational behavioral benchmark rather than a mechanistic definition). ([aima.cs.berkeley.edu](https://aima.cs.berkeley.edu/~russell/intro.html?utm_source=openai)) In philosophy of mind and interdisciplinary AI overviews, this agent/goal framing is contrasted with approaches that emphasize thought, cognition, embodiment, and normative standards like rationality and bounded optimality. ([kryten.mm.rpi.edu](https://kryten.mm.rpi.edu/SEP/index8.html?utm_source=openai))

A major line of foundational work asks whether intelligence can be **formally defined and measured** in a domain-general way. Legg & Hutter propose an information-theoretic/computational definition (“universal intelligence”) that scores an agent by its expected performance across a wide range of computable environments, linking intelligence to generalization, prediction, and decision-making rather than to any specific task battery (e.g., IQ-style tests). ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/cs/0605024?utm_source=openai)) Follow-on work explores approximations and practical “anytime” testing because the idealized measure is not directly computable. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1109.5951?utm_source=openai)) Together, these sources set up the core tension in a unifying theory: definitions that are broad and principled often become mathematically idealized, while definitions that are operational and measurable risk being narrow, anthropocentric, or benchmark-dependent.
