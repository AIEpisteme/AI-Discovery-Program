Across psychology and psychometrics, “intelligence” is often treated as a stable, measurable general mental capability (the *g* tradition), operationalized through performance on standardized tasks. Consensus-style statements (e.g., Gottfredson’s “Mainstream Science on Intelligence” and the APA task force report *Intelligence: Knowns and Unknowns*) emphasize abilities like reasoning, planning, problem solving, abstract thinking, learning quickly, and learning from experience—while also noting persistent scientific disputes about structure, causes, and appropriate interpretations of test scores. ([www1.udel.edu](https://www1.udel.edu/educ/gottfredson/reprints/1997mainstream.pdf?utm_source=openai))

In computer science and AI, definitions tend to be more explicitly functional and goal-directed. The influential “rational agent” framing (popularized in *Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach*) treats intelligence as behavior that selects actions expected to achieve goals under uncertainty and resource limits, shifting focus from human-likeness to performance. A parallel, more formal research line attempts mathematical definitions and measures of machine intelligence; Legg & Hutter’s “universal intelligence” proposal (grounded in algorithmic information theory and reinforcement-learning-style interaction with environments) distills many informal definitions into a quantitative notion of goal achievement across a wide range of environments. This highlights a recurring fault line: intelligence as *psychometric capacity* vs. *agentic competence* vs. *formal, environment-general performance*. ([aima.cs.berkeley.edu](https://aima.cs.berkeley.edu/~russell/intro.html?utm_source=openai))

Philosophy of mind and foundational AI add an additional layer: whether “intelligence” is best defined behaviorally, computationally, or in terms of mental states/conscious understanding. Turing’s 1950 “imitation game” is a classic operational proposal—sidestepping the definition of “thinking” by using indistinguishable linguistic behavior as a criterion—yet it also motivates enduring debates about whether passing behavioral tests captures the essence of intelligence or merely its outward signs. ([cse.msu.edu](https://www.cse.msu.edu/~cse841/papers/Turing.html?utm_source=openai))
